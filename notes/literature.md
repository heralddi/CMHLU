# Reading notes

These are working notes, not a finished literature review. I am using them to keep the modeling choices connected to pragmatic theory, L2 interpretation, and the syntax/semantics of embedded questions.

## RSA and pragmatic inference

- Frank and Goodman (2012) is the main entry point for the idea that listeners reason about why a speaker chose one utterance rather than another. The current model borrows this listener-speaker-listener structure, but uses a very small hand-built message set.
- Goodman and Frank (2016) is useful for seeing RSA as a family of models rather than a single fixed recipe. That matters here because the costs and priors are not yet estimated from data.

## L2 pragmatics and sentence interpretation

- Gricean pragmatics gives the broader motivation: listeners do not interpret an utterance only from literal truth conditions, but also from assumptions about speaker choices.
- Work on L2 pragmatic development suggests that non-native speakers may know the literal meanings of forms while still differing in production preferences, processing speed, or sensitivity to marked alternatives.
- For this project, the important question is not whether an L2 speaker literally has a separate grammar for `hope what S`. The smaller modeling question is whether a listener's assumptions about speaker background can change the interpretation of a marked utterance.

## Embedded wh-clauses and preferential predicates

- Predicates like `wonder` readily take embedded wh-clauses, while `hope` more naturally takes a finite declarative complement in standard English.
- The current notebook treats `hope what S` as a marked signal that still points toward a positive-desire state. That is a simplification, and it needs a better syntactic and semantic account before the model should be treated as more than exploratory.
- A useful next step is to compare `hope`, `fear`, `wonder`, and `know` with the same wh-clause frame, so the model is not built around only one example.
