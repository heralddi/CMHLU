# Reading notes

These are working notes, not a finished literature review. I am using them to keep the modeling choices connected to pragmatic theory, L2 interpretation, and the syntax/semantics of embedded questions.

## RSA and pragmatic inference

- Frank & Goodman (2012), *Science*, "Predicting Pragmatic Reasoning in Language Games." This is the basic reason I am modeling interpretation as a listener thinking about why a speaker chose one utterance rather than another.
- Goodman & Frank (2016), *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, "Pragmatic Language Interpretation as Probabilistic Inference." This helps me treat RSA as a flexible modeling style rather than a single fixed model.
- Degen (2023), *Annual Review of Linguistics*, "The Rational Speech Act Framework." This is useful for thinking about how costs, priors, and alternatives should be justified rather than simply hand-set.

## L2 pragmatics and sentence interpretation

- Kasper & Rose (2002), *Pragmatic Development in a Second Language.* This is a broad entry point for thinking about pragmatic development as separate from knowing individual word meanings.
- Slabakova (2010), *Second Language Research*, "Semantic Theory and Second Language Acquisition." This is useful background for separating semantic knowledge from processing or pragmatic use.
- Roberts, Gullberg, & Indefrey (2008), *Applied Psycholinguistics*, on online pronoun resolution in L2 sentence processing. I am using this as a reminder that L2 interpretation can differ even when the relevant words are familiar.
- For this project, the important question is not whether an L2 speaker literally has a separate grammar for `hope what S`. The smaller modeling question is whether a listener's assumptions about speaker background can change the predicted production or interpretation of a marked utterance.

## Embedded wh-clauses and preferential predicates

- Grimshaw (1979), "Complement Selection and the Lexicon," is a useful starting point for thinking about why different predicates select different complement types.
- Lahiri (2002), *Questions and Answers in Embedded Contexts*, gives background on embedded interrogatives and why they should not be treated as interchangeable with declarative complements.
- Predicates like `wonder` readily take embedded wh-clauses, while `hope` more naturally takes a finite declarative complement in standard English.
- The current notebook treats `hope what S` as a marked signal that still points toward a positive-desire state. That is a simplification, and it needs a better syntactic and semantic account before the model should be treated as more than exploratory.
- A useful next step is to compare `hope`, `fear`, `wonder`, and `know` with the same wh-clause frame, so the model is not built around only one example.
